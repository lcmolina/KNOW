e-publishing


systems & infrastructures

As technologies continue to move online, scholarship follows. This may be, in part, a result of the desire to make learning resources more available to alternative and independent educators and learners. In fact, the majority of systems and infrastructures developed as large repositories for academic journals and scholarly publishing have restricted their access behind expensive paywalls. Not only can this kind of infrastructure created by large repositories reinforce the need to belong to an institution, university, or college but it can also keep smaller databases from finding the funds to maintain up-to-date, relevant sites.

Despite disservice to learners outside of the spheres of higher education, academic libraries are pressured to stay attuned to the needs of students, faculty, and staff. What kind of decisions do librarians make when choosing subscriptions to big servers, like Elsevier? What do they look like? Will electronic resource management continue to be a requirement for the librarian or become an automated practice, freeing the librarian from administrative duties and allowing them to be more engaged with their institutions?
